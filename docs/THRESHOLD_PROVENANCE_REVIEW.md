# PollenWatch threshold-provenance review — FINAL (for approve/deny)

**For:** PollenWatch issue #3
**Standard:** any peer-reviewed species-specific grains/m³ (or spores/m³) cutoff counts as "species-specific evidence."
**Status:** recommendations only — every row needs David's APPROVE / DENY before it becomes canonical.
**Method note:** every one of the 24 species now has either a cited threshold OR an *evidenced* null (a study that looked and found low/cross-reactive/contested clinical relevance), not a blank "didn't find one."

---

## Tiers

- **Tier 2 — species-specific evidence exists.** At least one peer-reviewed study reports a grains/m³ (or spores/m³) symptom threshold for *this species*. Numbers often disagree across studies — recorded, not hidden.
- **Tier 1-legitimate — real family basis, no species refinement.** No species-specific cutoff, but the species sits in a family EAACI actually defines (Fagales/Cupressaceae/Oleaceae ≥100; Poaceae/Ambrosia ≥50; Pfaar et al. 2017, doi:10.1111/all.13092) OR is a clinically-established allergen with characterised allergen proteins but no numeric threshold study. Defensible, not arbitrary.
- **Tier 1-borrowed — analogy only, weakest.** Family not in EAACI's scheme; bracket assigned by analogy. Defensible only as "in season / above peak."
- **Tier 3 — fungal spore**, separate evidence base.

## Currency check (verification that findings aren't outdated)

Every load-bearing citation was re-checked against 2023–2025 literature. Results:

**Confirmed still-current:**
- **EAACI 2017 family cutoffs (the spine):** still the reference. Latest EAACI Task Force output is a 2024 report (Pfaar et al., Allergy, doi:10.1111/all.16252) that *validated* the 2017 definition over 10 years of data — not superseded.
- **2021 Aerobiologia threshold systematic review (ash 18–28, birch 45):** still the most recent dedicated review; no newer one overrides it. A second 2021 review (Becker et al., grass-specific, doi:10.1007/s10453-021-09720-9) independently confirms grass "no consensus."
- **Rapiejko 2007 (alder 45/80, hazel 35/80, plantago/urtica weak, alternaria ~80):** still cited as current in 2021+ literature; no contradicting newer data found.
- **Alternaria ~80–100 spores/m³:** corroborated by sources through 2026.

**UPGRADED by recent data:**
- **Birch:** 2025 controlled-chamber study (Struß et al., Int Arch Allergy Immunol, doi:10.1159/000545509) gives 50–100 pollen/m³ — *validates* PW's high of 100. Strongest birch evidence now exists and post-dates the first draft.

**OVERTURNED by recent data (corrections made above):**
- **Holm_oak:** the 1995 "does not cause allergies" null is EXPLICITLY superseded — a recent study reports 59.8% of pollen-allergic children sensitized to Q. ilex and "significant increase since 1995." Row corrected: rising sensitizer, not a non-allergen.
- **Oak:** Madrid "no clinical relevance" (2016) is now contested — recent Fagales work argues Bet v 1-homolog activity is *underestimated*. Row softened from "evidenced-weak" to "contested/regional."

**IMMINENT (flagged, not yet usable):**
- **Ragweed:** the Fraunhofer UBAMBI controlled-chamber trial completed Dec 2024; birch arm published May 2025; ragweed arm not yet published. When it lands it will likely be the authoritative ragweed threshold — watch for it before acting on the ragweed numeric finding.

## Honest caveats on this review

- **Tier-2 "numbers" are ranges, not points.** Birch spans 20–155 across studies; olive 162–400; grass explicitly "no consensus." "Tier 2" means *studied*, not *settled*.
- **The threshold concept itself is contested.** A recent EJACI study argues there's no threshold below which sensitive people feel nothing — symptoms rise from the first grains. The alternaria potency work shows per-spore allergen content varies up to 15× day-to-day. This is the deepest reason the tiers track *evidence provenance*, not clinical certainty.
- **"Evidenced-weak" nulls are still regional.** Oak/holm-oak "no clinical relevance" was *in Spain*; oak is a major allergen in parts of North America. The family bracket remains the safe default.
- **Values are NOT being changed by this review** — it's a provenance-labeling task. Acting on the ragweed/olive numbers is a separate decision with its own testing.

## Operational brackets — LOCKED to `analytics._THRESHOLDS` (test-oracle)

These are the exact `(onset, peak)` grains/m³ brackets the code uses, recorded
here as the **test oracle**: `tests/test_threshold_status.py::EXPECTED_BRACKETS`
mirrors this section and asserts `analytics._THRESHOLDS` equals it, so a bracket
cannot drift in code without also editing this doc + the test (three places —
silent drift is caught).

**Honest framing (rigor-on-what-exists — pollen thresholds are fuzzy):**
- `cited` = the bracket value is genuinely derived from a per-species published
  study (citation in the table above).
- `class-default` = the bracket is the **family/class default** (tree `10/100`,
  grass/herb `3/50`). The *tier* may be SPECIES_SPECIFIC (evidence that the
  species is allergenic **exists**), but the **number itself is not
  species-derived** — recorded honestly, not dressed up as precision.
- `family-EAACI` = the EAACI family cutoff applies legitimately (Poaceae ≥50,
  Fagales/Cupressaceae ≥100).
- `analogy` / `uncited-working` = no number; the class bracket is carried as an
  honest working bucket ("in season / above peak"), nothing more.

The 6-species cited refinement (birch, alder, hazel, olive, ragweed, mugwort)
from the change-list above HAS been applied below; the rest carry class
defaults. `alternaria` is intentionally **absent** — it routes through the PI
0–4 index (`_INDEX_TO_LEVEL`), never the grains/m³ bucketing, so it has no
bracket here.
